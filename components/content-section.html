Description: Transformer-based LLMs (Tb-LLMs) demonstrate extraordinary capabilities and, thus, change the approach of the KR communities to research. Large chunks of research topics are neglected as Tb-LLMs seem to be the ultimate solution. However, it seems that Tb-LLMs' capabilities depend on their ability to memorize (Carlini et al. (2021), Zhang et al. (2023), Carlini et al. (2023), Kim et al. (2023), Ranaldi et al. (2023)). Moreover, the necessity for deep neural networks to memorize long-tailed data to obtain close to optimal generalization error has attracted a lot of discussion (Feldman (2020), Feldman and Zhang (2020)). Then, a flourishing research area on LLMs is exploring how these models memorize and generalize from data. Understanding these facts is mandatory to investigate how these models can be understood with neuro-symbolic approaches and controlled through mechanistic interpretability methods.

Building on the long-standing debate on how neural networks encode discrete symbols, which are the bases of Tb-LLMs, it is now time to show again how neural networks on discrete symbols are holistic reasoners (Chalmers (1992)) over "classical" symbolic approaches (Fodor and Pylyshyn, 1988). This will give a profound understanding of the inner workings of Tb-LLMs and, thus, the ability to design novel architectures according to the possible, understood, and foreseen memorization and generalization capabilities of neural networks.

Although based on traditional research, this tutorial presents these ideas within the dominating paradigm of Tb-LLMs and then gives a profound understanding of the inner workings of these models, which will help practitioners build neuro-symbolic-and-mechanistic-interpretable-by-design-based LLMs.

Motivation: The SW community will benefit from this new light on existing methods as it will open novel perspectives on integrating explicit knowledge in LLMs based on novel Neural Network architectures.
